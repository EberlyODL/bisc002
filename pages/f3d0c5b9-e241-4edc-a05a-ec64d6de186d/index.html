In cases of Codominance there is no dominant and recessive pattern; things are more complicated than just two alleles. In codominance all alleles are expressed equally. A common example of this is the blood typing system. There are 4 blood types; A, B, AB and O. The blood type gene has more than just two alleles there are 3 alleles in this case and each one can be expressed on the red blood cells. However, a person can only have 2 of these 3 alleles, one coming from each parent.

A person can have an A allele, a B allele or an O allele. However, everyone has two of these alleles. So in the end, there are 6 different genotype possibilities:

1. AA
2. AB
3. AO
4. BB
5. BO
6. OO

A person with the A allele will have a marker placed on all their red blood cells that identifies the cell as a type A cell. If the person has the B allele as the second allele they will then be blood type AB as both markers are placed on all the red blood cells. A person with two O alleles would be type O blood. While the A allele codes for an A marker and the B allele codes for a B marker the O allele does not code for an O marker. The O allele means that no markers are placed on the red blood cell. The O allele means zero markers are used.

Trying to figure out punnett squares in this case becomes a bit trickier than standard dominance patterns. Sometimes phenotype and genotype are the same but other times one canâ€™t tell the genotype based on the phenotype. A person with type A blood could have two A alleles. Or the person could have one A and one O allele. They would still have type A blood. The same is true of type B blood, a person could have two B alleles or one B and one O allele. When doing punnett squares one has to be aware of both possibilities.
