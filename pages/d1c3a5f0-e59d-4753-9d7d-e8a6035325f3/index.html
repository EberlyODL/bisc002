The ancient Egyptians, Greeks and later Romans all made contributions to science in their day but many of their beliefs were still rooted in the supernatural world. All of those cultures had religious tradition of praying to many gods each of who were supposed to deal with a specific human concern. There were gods for healing, fertility, the harvest and success in war. Naturally many of their understandings of the natural world were attributed to the actions of one or more of the gods. For example, the sun traversed the sky each day on Apollo's chariot.

More modern scientific views were first developed during the Renaissance period of European history. During this time it became possible to begin to build sophisticated tools that allowed for the study of objects poorly understood at the time. Technology had advanced enough to create devices like telescopes and even microscopes to look at things either very far away or very small. Without being able to see object clearly, it is no wonder that the ancient Romans and Greeks relied on supernatural explanations for natural events.

So a certain level of technology had to be developed and new philosophies about the natural world came to light during the Renaissance. People were asking questions about nature rather than merely accepting the views that had existed up to that time. As a result a long string of visionaries came along and each contributed something to the development of modern science.
